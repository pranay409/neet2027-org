Master Classification, Phyla, and NCERT-Based Questions

The Animal Kingdom chapter is a cornerstone of NEET Biology, accounting for approximately 2-3 questions in the final exam. Understanding the classification system, characteristics of major phyla, and their distinguishing features is essential for securing full marks in this section. This comprehensive guide covers everything NEET 2027 aspirants need to know with exam-focused insights.

Fundamentals of Animal Classification

Animal classification follows a hierarchical system: Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species. The NEET exam expects students to master the Linnaean classification system and understand how animals are grouped based on specific morphological and anatomical characteristics.

The Kingdom Animalia is divided into two main groups based on body cavity (coelom) presence:

  • Non-Coelomata (Acoelomate): No true body cavity. Example: Platyhelminthes
  • Coelomata: Possess a true coelom lined by mesoderm

Coelomata animals are further classified into:

  • Protostomia: Blastopore develops into mouth (Phyla: Annelida, Arthropoda, Mollusca)
  • Deuterostomia: Blastopore develops into anus (Phyla: Echinodermata, Chordata)

Major Phyla: Characteristics and Examples

Porifera (Sponges)

  • Most primitive multicellular animals
  • Lack body symmetry (asymmetrical)
  • Possess specialized cells: choanocytes, archaeocytes, sclerocytes
  • Reproduction: asexual (budding) and sexual (gamete formation)
  • Example: Sycon, Euplectella

Cnidaria

  • Radially symmetrical animals
  • Possess cnidocytes (stinging cells) with nematocysts for prey capture
  • Gastrovascular cavity with single opening (mouth)
  • Life cycle includes polyp and medusa forms (in some)
  • Examples: Hydra, Corals, Sea anemones, Jellyfish
  • NEET frequently tests cnidocyte structure and function

Platyhelminthes (Flatworms)

  • First acoelomate animals with bilateral symmetry
  • Possess simple nervous system: nerve net and brain
  • Three classes: Turbellaria (free-living), Trematoda (parasitic), Cestoda (tapeworms)
  • Excretory system: flame cells
  • Example: Planaria (free-living), Fasciola (liver fluke)

Nematoda (Roundworms)

  • Pseudocoelomate animals with complete digestive system
  • Unsegmented body with cuticle
  • Primarily parasitic in humans and animals
  • Examples: Ascaris (intestinal parasite), Wuchereria (causes filariasis)
  • NEET emphasis: Ascaris life cycle and pathogenesis

Annelida (Segmented Worms)

  • First true coelomate animals with true coelom
  • Segmented body (metameric segmentation)
  • Closed circulatory system
  • Ventral nerve cord with ganglia
  • Classes: Polychaeta (marine), Oligochaeta (earthworms), Hirudinea (leeches)
  • Example: Nereis, Lumbricus terrestris (earthworm)

Arthropoda

  • Largest phylum with over 1 million described species
  • Exoskeleton composed of chitin
  • Jointed appendages (legs, antennae, mouthparts)
  • Open circulatory system
  • Classes: Insecta (insects), Arachnida (spiders, scorpions), Crustacea (crabs, shrimps), Myriapoda (centipedes, millipedes)
  • NEET focus: Insect anatomy, metamorphosis, and ecological importance

Mollusca

  • Soft-bodied animals with muscular foot
  • Shell secreted by mantle (in most)
  • Closed circulatory system (except in some)
  • Classes: Gastropoda (snails, slugs), Bivalvia (clams, mussels), Cephalopoda (octopus, squid)
  • Example: Pila (apple snail), Mytilus (mussel)

Echinodermata

  • Radially symmetrical adults (larva is bilateral)
  • Pentamerous body plan
  • Water vascular system unique to this phylum
  • Tube feet for locomotion and feeding
  • Classes: Asteroidea (starfish), Echinoidea (sea urchins), Holothuroidea (sea cucumbers)

Chordata

  • Characterized by notochord, dorsal hollow nerve cord, pharyngeal slits
  • Subphylum Vertebrata includes all vertebrate animals
  • Classes: Pisces (fish), Amphibia, Reptilia, Aves (birds), Mammalia
  • NEET heavily emphasizes vertebrate classification and characteristics
