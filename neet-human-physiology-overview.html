Human Physiology for NEET: The 8 Systems You Must Master

Biology • July 03, 2026

Human physiology accounts for 15-20% of the NEET Biology paper, making it one of the most scoring sections. Understanding the 8 major physiological systems is essential not only for securing marks but also for developing a conceptual foundation required for both paper sections and viva questions. This guide provides an NCERT-aligned roadmap to master human physiology systematically.

The 8 Major Physiological Systems: Overview and Exam Weightage

The human body functions as an integrated system of 8 major physiological mechanisms. Each system serves distinct functions but works in coordination with others. In NEET, examiners test your understanding at three levels: (1) structural anatomy paired with function, (2) physiological processes and their regulation, and (3) clinical/practical applications.

1. Nervous System

~12-15 marks | Central and peripheral coordination

2. Muscular System

~8-10 marks | Movement and posture regulation

3. Circulatory System

~15-18 marks | Transport and circulation

4. Respiratory System

~10-12 marks | Gas exchange mechanisms

5. Digestive System

~12-15 marks | Nutrient absorption and metabolism

6. Urinary/Excretory System

~10-12 marks | Waste removal and osmoregulation

7. Endocrine System

~12-15 marks | Hormonal regulation

8. Reproductive System

~8-10 marks | Reproduction and gametogenesis

⚡ NEET Strategy Tip

Focus Areas: Circulatory, Nervous, and Digestive systems carry the highest weightage (40-45 marks combined). Master these first, then allocate time proportionally to other systems.

Question Pattern: Expect 60% MCQs testing mechanism understanding, 30% comparative questions between systems, 10% diagram-based and clinical scenario questions.

The Nervous System: Control and Coordination (NCERT Class 11, Chapter 21)

NCERT Coverage: Structure of neuron, nerve impulse transmission, reflex arc, brain anatomy, spinal cord functions, autonomic nervous system

The nervous system is tested extensively in NEET through multiple angles. Examiners focus on three critical areas: (1) structural identification—naming parts of neurons, brain regions, and spinal cord segments; (2) functional mechanisms—action potentials, synaptic transmission, and reflex arcs; and (3) integration questions linking nervous system disorders to physiological dysfunction.

Key Concepts to Master:

  • Neuron Structure and Classification: Understand multipolar, bipolar, and unipolar neurons with their functional roles. Practice drawing and labeling neurons, identifying axons, dendrites, and synaptic terminals.
  • Nerve Impulse Transmission: Memorize the sequence of events in action potential (resting potential -70mV, depolarization, repolarization, hyperpolarization). NEET frequently asks comparative questions: "At which phase can another stimulus trigger a response?"
  • Synaptic Plasticity and Neurotransmitters: Know the 8-10 major neurotransmitters (acetylcholine, dopamine, serotonin, GABA) and their roles. Questions often pair neurotransmitter dysfunction with disorders.
  • Brain Regions and Functions: Memorize the 5 major brain divisions, their subdivisions, and specific functions. Example question: "Damage to which part causes loss of temperature regulation?" (Hypothalamus)
  • Reflex Arc Components: Know the 5 components in order and which are conscious/unconscious. Spinal reflex questions are common assertion-reason type.
  • Autonomic Nervous System: Compare sympathetic vs. parasympathetic effects on heart rate, pupil size, digestion—expect 2-3 specific comparison questions.

Circulatory and Respiratory Systems: Transport and Gas Exchange (NCERT Class 11, Chapters 18-19)

NCERT Coverage: Heart anatomy, cardiac cycle, ECG, blood composition, coronary circulation, respiratory mechanics, lung volumes, oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation

These two systems are interconnected functionally and are tested together in application-based questions. NEET expects deep understanding of: cardiac physiology (pressure changes during systole/diastole), lung mechanics (Boyle's law application), and oxygen transport mechanisms.

Circulatory System—Critical Topics:

  • Cardiac Cycle and Pressure Variations: Know the exact sequence of atrial/ventricular contractions and corresponding pressure changes. Questions ask: "At what point does the aortic valve open?" or "Which sound corresponds to which valve closure?"
  • ECG Interpretation: Understand P wave (atrial depolarization), QRS complex (ventricular depolarization), T wave (ventricular repolarization). Predict abnormalities from ECG changes.
  • Heart Rate Regulation: Study the cardiac center in the medulla, sympathetic/parasympathetic control, and hormonal effects (epinephrine increases HR).
  • Blood Composition and Clotting: Know plasma protein functions, RBC lifespan (120 days), WBC types and ratios (normal TLC 4500-11000/μL), and clotting cascade steps.

Respiratory System—Critical Topics:

  • Lung Volumes and Capacities: Memorize TLC = 6000mL, VC = 4800mL, FRC = 2400mL. Practice calculation questions: "If TV = 500mL and IRV = 3100mL, calculate IC."
  • Mechanism of Respiration: Understand pressure gradient-driven airflow. Know that inspiration increases thoracic volume (Boyle's Law: P ∝ 1/V), and expiration reverses this.
  • Oxygen-Hemoglobin Dissociation Curve: Understand the sigmoidal shape, P50 (26mmHg), and factors shifting the curve (temperature, pH, 2,3-DPG). Questions ask: "At which pO2 is Hb 50% saturated?"
  • CO₂ Transport and Respiratory Regulation: Know 3 forms of CO₂ transport: dissolved (5%), carbaminohemoglobin (20%), bicarbonate (75%). Study the chemoreceptor role in ventilation control.

Digestive, Endocrine, and Reproductive Systems: Metabolism and Regulation (NCERT Class 11-12, Chapters 16, 22)

NCERT Coverage: GI tract histology, enzyme secretion and regulation, nutrient absorption, hormone synthesis and action, gametogenesis, menstrual cycle, reproductive anatomy

These three systems control metabolism, growth, and reproduction. NEET tests your ability to connect structure with function and predict physiological responses to stimuli.

Digestive System—Exam-Focused Areas:

  • Enzyme Specificity and pH Optima: Know enzyme names, sources, substrates, optimal pH, and cofactors. Pepsin works at pH 1.5-2 (stomach), trypsin at pH 8 (small intestine). Questions ask: "Which enzyme is inactive in the stomach?"
  • Regulation of Secretions: Understand neural (vagus nerve) and hormonal control (gastrin, CCK, secretin). Know that gastrin increases HCl secretion, CCK stimulates pancreatic enzyme release.
  • Nutrient Absorption Sites: Most absorption occurs in the small intestine. Know specific absorption: glucose (active transport), fatty acids (chylomicrons), iron (duodenum), B12 (terminal ileum).
  • Liver Functions: Master 4-5 major roles: plasma protein synthesis, detoxification, bile production, nutrient metabolism. Be ready for application questions about liver cirrhosis effects.
